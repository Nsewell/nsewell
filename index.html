Phil Zuckerman on the Basis for a Civil Society

Nov 1st, 2013

Phil Zuckerman recently took part in a debate with David Marshall on whether religion or secularism is a better basis for a civil society. After initially refusing to post the video online because they thought Zuckerman won, the church caved and the video is up.

Liquid error: No space left on device - /tmp/youtube-cache.json

Zuckerman stuck to the facts about why largely secular post-Chrsitian societies around the world are doing better on most indicators than the most religious societies, while Marshall presented weak anecdotes of individual conversion experiences and straw men about how communists and vikings are bad people. The pastor of the church said “It just didn’t go the way we wanted it to go. We were not represented well.”

Zuckerman has noted that many European societies that score well on social indicators also have low rates of Chrsitian belief. Only 18% of Swedes believe in heaven, 10% believe in hell and 3% believe that the Bible is the literal word of God, among the lowest rates in the world. In Denmark, women’s labour force participation doubled in the ’70s and ’80s, rapidly changing the model of femininity valued by young women. Social changes like these supported by neoliberal governments, including the banning of prosletyzing in schools, and the provision of free healthcare to all have quickly caused generational decline in religion as the state Lutheran church struggles to market itself.

Zuckerman eloquently criticised the bible belt states for high rates of divorce, teen pregnancy, sexual assault, diabetes, child abuse, unemployment and murder. He has a long history of criticising the political hypocrisy of the white evangelical power base, that claims evolution is wrong, but tend to support policies that could be described as “Social Darwinism.”

The results from a recent poll published by the Pew Forum on Religion and Public Life reveal what social scientists have known for a long time: White Evangelical Christians are the group least likely to support politicians or policies that reflect the actual teachings of Jesus.

Zuckerman has an excellent analysis of the global decline of faith on Edge.org. One interesting fact about the general decline of Chrstianity in America is how gender plays a factor in mixed faith households:

Rather than Amerofaith becoming deeply patriarchal as Longman thinks, it is increasingly feminine. Women church goers greatly outnumber men, who find church too dull. Here’s the kicker. Children tend to pick up their beliefs from their fathers. So, despite a vibrant evangelical youth cohort, young Americans taken as a whole are the least religious and most culturally tolerant age group in the nation.

He notes that the well resourced churches are consolidating believers rather than expanding the faithful

Even the megachurch phenomenon is illusory. A spiritual cross of sports stadiums with theme parks, hi-tech churches are a desperate effort to pull in and satisfy a mass-media jaded audience for whom the old sit in the pews and listen to the standard sermon and sing some old time hymns does not cut it anymore. Rather than boosting church membership, megachurches are merely consolidating it.

While Zuckerman notes that he was well remunerated for travelling to take part in the professionally recorded debate, the decision of the church to initially refuse to publish the video demonstrates that content matters more than its presentation. The trend of religion being on the nose for the last hundred years will continue as churches struggle to cover up their mistakes in an age of social media, no matter how well they learn to speak with ‘Gen Y’.

The Bishop of Bling Exposing Resentment of Catholicism

Oct 25th, 2013

The pope has banished the “Bishop of Bling” Franz-Peter Tebartz-van Elst over allegations of lying.

Franz-Peter Tebartz-van Elst announced that his diocean centre would cost 5.5 million euros in 2010, but the architect admitted that the true cost was known to be much higher from the start, with plans for extravagant addons like half a million euros for art and 20,000 for a bathtub causing the project to blow out to 32 million euros. This came at a time when his staff were facing funding cuts because of a savings plan instituted a principle of austerity in the church.

The pope has instituted financial reforms in the Vatican to encourage transparency and accountability, and to prevent money laundering. German dioceses are chafing under the new plans, which would reveal to their donors just how well-off they are.

As reported in the Religion News Service, German dioceses are among the world’s welthiest, and the diocese of Cologne is richer than the Vatican. By law, Germans registered as Catholics must give 8-10% of their tax revenue to the church, which gave the church $7.1 US billion in 2012. Even as over 100,000 members leave the church every year following child sex abuse scandals, the dioceses still have enormous asset reserves.

While the new Argentinian pope travells on a bus or in a donated 20 year old Renault his obstinate German subordinantes refuse to give up their BMWs and Audis. The previous pope took up a yearly collection of $200 million US, which will certainly guarantee him a comfortable life in retirement, while the new pope has opted to live in a single room in a guesthouse rather than his papal apartment.

“The German Catholic Church is one of the country’s wealthiest and largest organizations and its top officials expect a certain lifestyle,” said Carsten Frerk, who has published two books on the German churches’ wealth and what he describes as their opaque financing. “But they are wary of the extent of their wealth becoming broadly known because it might lead to fewer donations.”

Francis has commented that he dislikes careerist priests, stating “It also happens to me that when I meet a clericalist, I suddenly become anti-clerical. Clericalism should not have anything to do with Christianity.”

Francis had stated in September that the Catholic Church was too focussed on sexual issues like homosexuality, abortion and contraception, and they must increase their focus on the poor and marginalised. This has apparently had little effect in the US, where the US Conference of Catholic Bishops campaigned for Republicans to introduce religious priviliges into the ‘must pass’ resolution to end the government shutdown and avert a US government default. The shutdown cost 800,000 jobs and left poor families without food stamps for weeks, and resulted in the Republican party hitting the lowest approval rating of any political party recorded by Gallup. The government shutdown over Obamacare appears to be a long-planned offensive from the USCCB, with their opposition to Obamacare parroted by the Romney campaign.

Tony Abbott Messes With ACT’s Marriage Laws

Oct 11th, 2013

When Tony Abbott was asked about his views on same sex marriage in the leader’s debate, he replied with this:

“It’s a very important issue. I know how important an issue it is. It’s not the only important issue and I’ve got to say as far as an incoming Coalition government is concerned, the priority will be on things like reducing cost-of-living pressure and increasing job security.”

Liquid error: No space left on device - /tmp/youtube-cache.json

That must have been a “non-core” promise, given that George Brandis is almost straight away working to overrule a law on same sex marriage the ACT wants to bring in. George Brandis has claimed that the ACT Bill would undermine the ‘well-established position’ of ‘nationally consistent marriage laws.’ The Tasmanian Law Reform commission suggests that this is possibly not the case, with a history of states passing their own marriage laws, so it’s unknown if the ACT bill will be found constitutional. Politifact rates Brandis’s statement as half true.

While Abbott noted in the debate that same sex marriage was an “important” issue for his lesbian sister, his view on the issue has not “evolved” but goes against the majority of the Australian population. Figures from the ABC’s Vote Compass survey of 422,000 respondents show that the majority of Australians support same sex marriage, and 70% of those under 34 do. This is in line with previous opinion polls finding that about 60% of the country now supports same sex marriage.

There’s an old quote “every time you hear an ambulance siren, an opponent of gay rights is leaving the world or a supporter is entering it”. In reality, with NZ and the UK passing gay marriage laws it’s already a lost argument. The UK law will come into effect in 2014, I don’t think the Christian lobby can hold the issue back that much longer.
