The Media Still Pushing Debunked Gerson Therapy

Jan 30th, 2014

Jessica Ainscough is working though a list of patsies, finding her way into being reported (with the barest of skepticism) by the Sydney Morning Herald. I wrote about her unfortunate dependance on quackery in June, and her mother has since passed away. Orac notes that as Ainscough’s mother was dying, she claimed that the tumours were “healing reactions” and there was an underlying “copper toxicity” supposedly treatable with chelation therapy:

How many times have we heard cancer quacks say this, that the cancer is not the problem but rather a “symptom” of the “real” problem or a “protective reaction” to the real problem?

As I noted in June, Ainscough invented “psychic pollution”

our negative thoughts also contribute to pollution on our planet. This is called ‘psychic pollution.’ This fact has been proven: what we think and feel has a direct impact on the world around us

There are many benefits to encouraging cancer patients to seek professional counselling services when dealing with the side effects of radiation and chemotherapy, as well as depression resulting from their diagnosis. Unfortunately, many quacks try to adopt the language of pop-psychology and tell people with terminal cancer that their depression is the cause of their terminal condition, and they need to force themselves to think positively in order to be cured. Not only will terminal patients go to the grabe believing that they have some spiritual or mental deficiency that causes their condition, but quacks are given an easy excuse for the failures of treatments on patients believed to be too “weak” or “faithless”.
