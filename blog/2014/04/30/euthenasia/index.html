Marketing of Euthenasia

Apr 30th, 2014

Many driving safety advertising campaigns use shocking images of death and disfigurement to drive safely, obey the road rules and put on a seat belt. The ‘embrace life’ campaign has avoided shock and horror and instead opted for a positive message about seatbelts:

Embrace Life - always wear your seat belt

WARNING: All rights of the producer and of the owner of the work reserved. Unauthorised copying, public performance, radio or tv broadcasting of this material is prohibited. Music by Siddhartha Barnhoorn - http://www.sidbarnhoorn.com An original approach to road safety advertising from the Sussex Safer Roads Partnership. http://www.sussexsaferroads.gov.uk Join the Facebook group for Embrace Life at http://www.tinyurl.com/embracetv

People often try to put thoughts of death and suffering out of their minds, as they cause anguish and consternation. Positive messages are more likely to be talked about among families and peer-groups.

The voluntary euthanasia campaign has had verry little success in getting their message across.

Here’s a list of some groups / issues currently out-performing [voluntary euthanasia]:

  • live cattle;
  • puppies born in puppy farms;
  • asylum seekers;
  • transgendered people;
  • homosexuals;
  • the disabled;
  • victims of domestic violence;
  • the corral in the barrier reef;
  • farmers affected by fracking;
  • victims of sexual abuse at the hands of clergy.

These groups are a laundry list of disenfranchised, under-resourced, abused, neglected and unorganised – yet each of them outperform [voluntary euthanasia] week-in, week-out in the battle for media space, barbeque discussions and political response.

The ‘pro-choice’ movement that advocates for a woman’s right to choose abortion (or not) is careful to state that they are not ‘pro-abortion’. They don’t prescribe the circumstances under which they believe a woman should seek an abortion, they leave the choice to the individual. The label ‘Dying with Dignity’ may not be a good name for the voluntary euthanasia movement, and the group ’Doctors for Voluntary Euthanasia Choice’, are approaching a better label.

Death and suffering are taboo because the thought of them cuases distress, so the voluntary euthanasia movement needs to find positive ways to communicate their message. Additionally, the voluntary euthanasia movement has focussed on ultra-leftist audiences to the detriment of broad community outreach.
