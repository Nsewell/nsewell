I Read Halfway Though This Article and Literally Couldn't Believe What I Was Reading... Amazing!

Mar 15th, 2014

As Voltaire once said “The secret of being a bore is to tell everything,” and the secret to a good story is to get the listener to care about the exposition before you reveal the conclusion. Many of the videos that go viral online are professionally produced pieces that fool the viewers into sharing the video with friends without realising that it’s an ad. Recently millions of people shared an artsy black and white video where actors pretended to kiss people for the first time, which was really an ad for a boring clothing company.

Last year Saatchi came up with this piece of slo-mo genius:

First Taste
Tasty video bit at #TEDxSydney 2013. Video by Saatchi & Heckler

Ads that go viral commonly focus on universal human truths that work in any language, on any continent. Many of the popular “best of web” videos focus on stunning nature scenes, slo-mo extreme sports and people with weird dance moves.

The parodies of the first kiss video are many and varied, this one with dogs is one of the stranger ones:

FIRST SNIFF (First Kiss Parody)
We asked some dogs to kiss for the first time…

Vice created a more realistically awkward video by getting 20 strangers who aren’t models to kiss each other. As Vice notes, a growing number of websites are getting better at tweaking algorithms to pump traffic-magnet content like “Half Way Through These Photos, I Could Barely Handle It. But I’m Glad I Made It to the End… Amazing.” As each new meme website tries to game social algorithms by running other people’s content under misleading headlines, more and more stories are just beat-ups created for the online outrage factory. One of the most egregious examples of race baiting is the bogus story that Chinese people are grinding up babies into soup to increase their sexual potency. In the race to reach the lowest common denominator, no barrier is too low for a quick buck.

Vice notes that there was a time when ad agencies veered on the side of overestimating the intelligence of their customer base, which was before the days of social media marketing managers. Just see Corporate Brand(R) on Facebook for fantastic ideas about user engagement. For every retweet that the douchebags get, they’ll even donate up to the fixed amount that they already decided to donate.

Sometimes people make honest mistakes by repeating accidental misquotes, and indeed one of the most famous Voltaire quotes wasn’t his. Others are trying to make money using the internet to peddle bunk that’s been around for centuries:
