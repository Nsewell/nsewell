The Moralising Effect of the Disgust Reflex

May 24th, 2014

I’ve written before about how [eye tracking can predict political preferences[(/blog/2014/04/05/eye-tracking). People who put more effort into actively avoiding disgusting images are more likely to be politically conservative:

Tracking which images you look at can predict your disgust response, and your political preference

I absolutely loved reading Jonathan Haidt’s The Righteous Mind - why good pepole are divided by religion and politics, which makes a strong argument that our genetics influence our political leanings. David Pizarro and Jonathan Haidt have built upon earlier work on the disgust reflex to explore how the reflex to spit out impure food is triggered when we think of disgusting sex practices, or “unclean” people. Haidt ran a study where he asked Americans how they would feel if an old woman needed a rag to clean a toilet, and she ripped up an old American flag she didn’t want. Conservatives were furious at the sacrilige of tearing up a flag in the privacy of her own house, while liberals argued that the community should tolerate a woman choosing how to use her own flag inside her own house. Another study asked how people would feel about a 34 year old woman masturbating while cuddling her favourite teddy bear. People who felt disgusted often put forward religious arguments about the design of the body. Pizarro, in a follow up, found that people were more likely to be judgemental of the unusual sex practice when standing next to a reminder to wash their hands:

David Pizarro: The strange politics of disgust
What does a disgusting image have to do with how you vote? Equipped with surveys and experiments, psychologist David Pizarro demonstrates a correlation between sensitivity to disgusting cues – a photo of feces, an unpleasant odor – and moral and political conservatism.
